**Why these features matter**

1. **Alcohol** – Alcohol is a strong indicator of fermentation vigor and body; higher levels typically correlate with richer, more complex wines, thus driving the model toward the “high‑quality” class.
2. **Sulphates** – Sulphates help preserve wine and contribute to a cleaner, more balanced taste. Higher sulphate concentrations are therefore rewarded by the classifier.
3. **Total / free sulfur dioxide** – Excess sulfur dioxide, especially when present as free SO₂, can impart off‑flavors and is often seen in lower‑quality wines. The model learns this negative association.
4. **Volatile acidity** – High volatile acidity signals over‑acidification or spoilage, so the model penalizes it for high‑quality predictions.
5. **Citric acid** – Acts as a natural preservative and buffer, improving taste stability; its positive contribution reflects that effect.
6. **Density** – Related to sugar and alcohol content; denser wines usually have higher alcohol and lower water content, aligning with quality expectations.
7. **pH** – Affects color stability and microbial stability. The interactions show that when pH is balanced with other factors (e.g., high sulphates), quality predictions improve.

**Actionable insights for winemakers**

- **Increase alcohol and sulphate levels** within optimal ranges to strengthen high‑quality predictions.
- **Monitor and keep total & free sulfur dioxide** below thresholds that start to reduce quality scores.
- **Control volatile acidity** during fermentation to prevent negative quality signals.
- **Leverage citric acid and pH management**: balanced citric acid with a suitable pH can boost quality.
- **Consider pairwise adjustments**: e.g., when chlorides are high, aim to reduce them or adjust residual sugar to offset the negative impact.
