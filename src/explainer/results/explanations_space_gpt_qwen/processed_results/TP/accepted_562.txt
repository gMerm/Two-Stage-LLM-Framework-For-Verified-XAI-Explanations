### Why these features matter

* **Alcohol** – Alcohol is a key indicator of wine strength and body; wines with higher alcohol content are typically judged as higher quality.  
* **Sulphates** – Sulphates are used in winemaking to balance acidity and add complexity; higher levels are often linked to more refined wines.  
* **Total sulfur dioxide** – SO₂ acts as a preservative; a moderate to high level helps maintain wine quality by preventing spoilage.  
* **Volatile acidity** – Low volatile acidity (less “sour” or “acetic” flavor) is a hallmark of well‑made wine.  
* **Citric acid** – Adds freshness and balance; higher citric acid generally improves sensory quality.  
* **Density** – Lower density corresponds to a lighter, more aromatic wine, often judged higher.  
* **Residual sugar** – High residual sugar can indicate a sweeter, less balanced wine, so lower sugar favors quality.  
* **Chlorides** – Saltiness can detract from flavor; lower chloride levels are preferable for quality.

These interactions capture subtle chemical balances that single‑feature models miss, helping the EBM achieve stronger discrimination between high‑ and low‑quality wines.
