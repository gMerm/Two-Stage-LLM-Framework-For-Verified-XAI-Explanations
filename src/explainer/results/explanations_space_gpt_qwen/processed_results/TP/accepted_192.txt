### What these numbers mean
* **Magnitude** – The value tells how much the feature (or interaction) helps the model discriminate between the two classes. Alcohol, for example, accounts for the largest share of predictive power.
* **Direction** – The supplied output does not include the sign of the effect. Thus we cannot say from this data alone whether higher or lower values push a wine toward high or low quality. (In practice, wine experts know that higher alcohol and sulphates often correlate with higher quality, but that inference is outside the provided numbers.)

### Why these features matter for wine quality
1. **Alcohol** – Contains the most information about a wine’s maturity and balance.  
2. **Sulphates** – Reflects the wine’s antioxidant and preservative status, often linked to flavor stability.  
3. **Total sulfur dioxide** – Indicates the amount of preservative sulfites added, affecting taste and spoilage resistance.  
4. **Volatile acidity** – Measures acidity that can be sharp or off‑flavor; higher levels usually lower perceived quality.  
5. **Citric acid** – Adds freshness and can counterbalance acidity.  
6. **Density** – Relates to sugar concentration and alcohol; useful as a proxy for overall composition.  
7. **Chlorides** – Salinity indicator; excessive chlorides can spoil flavor.  
8. **Residual sugar** – Balances acidity and contributes to body and sweetness.  
9. **Free sulfur dioxide** – Provides real‑time preservative capacity; important for preventing spoilage.

- **Citric acid & pH** – The combined acidity profile can better capture taste balance.

- **Total sulfur dioxide & sulphates**, **pH & sulphates**, **Total sulfur dioxide & density**, **Free sulfur dioxide & pH** – Each captures how preservative levels, acidity, and wine density intertwine to influence quality.
